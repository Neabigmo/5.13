\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Algorithmic stability is often introduced through a single intuitive gesture:
change one training example and ask whether the learner changes much. For
global smooth learners this slogan is usually harmless. For local rules such as
deterministic $k$-nearest neighbors, it is already underspecified. One can
delete an occurrence, append a new occurrence, replace an occurrence by an
adversarially chosen labeled point, or delete an occurrence and then evaluate
the modified classifier exactly at that removed occurrence, as in leave-one-out
(LOO) validation. These operations are related, but they are not identical. A
substantial amount of avoidable confusion enters the discussion when they are
treated as interchangeable.

This paper is about making those distinctions explicit in the smallest setting
where they become mathematically sharp: deterministic $k$-NN on finite metric
spaces with ordered samples, duplicate occurrences allowed, conflicting labels
allowed, and tie-breaking frozen once and for all. The finite setting strips
away distributional averaging and exposes the local combinatorics directly. In
that regime we can ask a very concrete question: when does a single
perturbation change the prediction, and what kinds of perturbations are
provably stronger than others?

\Cref{fig:stability-notions} previews the perspective. Delete-one,
add-one, replace-one, and LOO belong to the same family, but they move along
different axes. Replace-one is strongest at the operation level because it can
be factored into a deletion followed by an insertion; LOO is special because it
evaluates at the removed occurrence itself. Once these axes are disentangled,
two kinds of results emerge. First, there is a clean \emph{uniform calculus}:
replace-one control can be upper bounded by delete-one plus add-one control.
Second, there are unexpectedly sharp \emph{pointwise separations}: a sample can
be perfectly LOO-stable while still being highly unstable under replace-one.

The smallest explicit witness already appears for deterministic $1$-NN on a
two-vertex graph metric. In that example, deleting either occurrence does not
change the loss at the deleted occurrence, but replacing one occurrence by an
adversarially labeled duplicate flips the prediction at a chosen query point.
The witness is tiny enough to verify by hand, yet it already captures the main
moral of the paper: LOO and replace-one are not merely different formalizations
of the same local phenomenon.

Our contributions are therefore best read as a controlled bundle rather than a
single theorem.
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,label=(\arabic*)]
    \item We formalize the perturbation vocabulary for deterministic finite
    $k$-NN, with explicit conventions for ordered samples, duplicate
    occurrences, conflicting labels, and tie-breaking.
    \item We prove a uniform perturbation relation showing that replace-one can
    be decomposed into delete-one plus add-one at the loss level.
    \item We derive an exact signed-margin criterion for when a deterministic
    $k$-NN prediction changes after a perturbation. This makes the instability
    mechanism transparent: the label vote inside the ordered top-$k$ neighborhood
    must cross zero.
    \item We give an explicit finite metric separation for deterministic $1$-NN:
    pointwise LOO stability does not control pointwise replace-one stability.
    The witness itself is theorem-level; the current minimality claim remains a
    computational certificate.
    \item We provide reproducible odd-$k$ gadget candidates and publication
    assets that keep theorem statements, computational certificates, and
    conjectural lifting patterns cleanly separated.
\end{enumerate}

The paper is intentionally modest about what it does \emph{not} establish. We
do not claim a new generalization bound. We do not claim that the current
odd-$k$ search outputs constitute a theorem. We do not claim that finite
worst-case instability contradicts classical consistency results for nearest
neighbors. Instead, we offer a definition-level and mechanism-level account of
single-perturbation behavior that can live compatibly alongside the classical
distributional theory.

The reader's map is simple. \Cref{sec:related-work} positions the paper
relative to classical algorithmic stability, deleted-estimate, and conformal
