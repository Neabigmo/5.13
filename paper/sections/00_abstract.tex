\begin{abstract}
Algorithmic stability asks how much a learning rule changes when its training
sample is perturbed. For local rules such as nearest neighbors, however, the
phrase ``change one sample point'' hides several inequivalent operations:
deleting an occurrence, replacing it by an adversarial labeled point,
appending an occurrence, or deleting an occurrence and evaluating at that same
occurrence as in leave-one-out validation. We develop a finite-sample
stability calculus for deterministic $k$-nearest neighbors on finite metric
spaces under explicit tie-breaking. The paper separates uniform perturbation
relations from pointwise local phenomena: replace-one perturbations admit a
clean delete-plus-add decomposition at the operation level, while leave-one-out
stability need not control pointwise replace-one stability even for a two-point
graph metric. We give an exact signed-margin characterization of when a
deterministic $k$-NN prediction changes under a perturbation, visualize the
resulting instability mechanisms, and provide reproducible finite-graph witness
and certificate artifacts. Our odd-$k$ extension currently remains
computational evidence rather than theorem-level lifting. These finite
worst-case separations complement, rather than contradict, the classical
distributional consistency theory of nearest-neighbor rules.
\end{abstract}
